# Call with Will Gregory (Advisor)

Advisory conversation on university philanthropy culture and the non-profit
vs for-profit question.

### UK vs US Giving Culture

- UK university philanthropy is not part of the culture the way it is in the US, where it's treated as the alumni norm from day one
- Major UK donors are disproportionately Brits who built wealth in the US and adopted that giving culture there
- Oxford is financially inefficient and not cash-rich; wants to raise £125bn overall
- US universities have teams dedicated purely to small-scale donations; Oxford does not

### Working With Universities

- Blunt warning: "I do not recommend working with universities" — procurement cycles rival government, admin is slow/under-resourced, tenure breeds institutional stagnation
- His own initiative took 3 years to get approved by Oxford
- Colleges, departments and the university itself compete internally for the same donor funding — engagement requires navigating discrete, gatekept processes

### For-Profit vs Non-Profit Structure

- Recommends staying for-profit; there's a clear for-profit model for Dono
- "Don't obfuscate — we are a social venture" — avoid presenting as a non-profit when we're not
- **Fiscal sponsorship** flagged as the fallback if tax-receipt demand emerges: an existing non-profit with an aligned mission sponsors the for-profit, takes a small cut (a few %), and donations pass through with tax-deductible status — legally permitted, many organisations offer this, but some are partisan so vetting matters
- Small donations (~£100–£1,000) aren't meaningfully influenced by tax deductibility — not worth chasing non-profit status for this alone
- Relevant precedent: DonorsChoose (donorschoose.org), a non-profit with a similar product to Dono

### Other Notes

- Priming smaller donors toward larger future contributions is a recognised pattern worth designing for
- Network effects compound — spend time around ambitious people; YC has partners specifically focused on British founders/talent
