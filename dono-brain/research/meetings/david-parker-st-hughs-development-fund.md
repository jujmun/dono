# Meeting with David Parker (St Hugh's Development Fund)

Thu, 09 Jul 26

### Oxford Fundraising Landscape Overview

- University fundraising is structurally fragmented: colleges, central teams, museums/libraries, and sports all fundraise independently
- Colleges target their own alumni; central university targets companies, trusts, and foundations
- Core tension: Oxford looks wealthy from the outside, making the case for support harder than Cancer Research UK’s clear, universal ask
- Relationship management is college-led: central teams must ask permission before approaching a college’s alumni
  - Colleges can refuse if they’re already cultivating that individual, creating internal friction
- Alumni affinity at Oxford is unusually high: open rates and gift rates above most universities due to the intensity of the student experience

### Fundraising Mechanisms

- Telephone campaigns: most colleges run annual telethons; St. Hugh’s has run theirs since 2006
  - Cost: upwards of £50,000 with external consultants; can be done cheaper in-house
  - A good year yields £150,000–£300,000 (includes pledged future income, not just cash received)
  - Primary value is alumni engagement, not just income
- Giving days: growing in popularity, typically timed around Giving Tuesday (November)
  - Run on platforms like Hubbub; much cheaper than telethons
  - Key insight: headline totals often include large matched funding already in the bank (e.g., £600k day may have started with £200–300k pre-committed)
- Physical mailings: increasingly expensive (~£5/brochure), low return; now used only for highly targeted asks
  - Example: travel fund appeal sent only to Italian language graduates
- Legacy giving: high-value but hard to justify a dedicated officer; can yield enormous returns
  - Example: one legacy appeal letter returned a house on Woodstock Road worth ~£6M

### Major Gifts and Endowment Strategy

- Endowment is the primary long-term goal: draw ~4% annually, fund grows indefinitely
  - Example: £2M endows a fellowship (~£70–80k/year), covering an academic’s full cost forever
- St. John’s endowment cited as benchmark: roughly doubled in ~12 years, now approaching £800M
- Major gift cycle is long: 3–6 months to write a proposal for a 7–8 figure gift
  - Requires input from academics, estates teams, and senior leadership
  - Gifts typically pledged over 3–5 years
- Naming rights are the key lever for large gifts: buildings, posts, chairs named after donors
  - Examples: Blavatnik School of Government, Schwarzman Centre, Ertegun House
  - Naming assumed to last ~20–30 years before needing renewal (inflation erodes the original sum)
- Development directors spend most of their time on a prospect portfolio of ~100 people
  - Realistically only ~30 meaningful conversations per year
  - KPIs: emails sent, meeting requests, meetings held, proposals sent, gifts received, total value

### Donor Pipeline and Giving Culture

- Donor lifecycle: research → cultivate → solicit → steward → re-research
- Peak giving historically at ~50: highest earnings, children out of the house; cost-of-living pressures have flattened this curve
- Early giving sets the tone: even £10/month from a recent graduate builds lifetime giving habit
  - America is ~20–30 years ahead; Harvard MBA class gifts are expected as a norm
  - Leavers’ gifts and returners’ dinners (1 year post-graduation) cited as effective hooks
- Donor recognition tiers exist but rarely drive giving on their own
  - Exception: if a tier unlocks something tangible (e.g., annual dinner with the Vice-Chancellor)
- Suggested ask amounts matter: symbolic numbers work (e.g., £700 for Oriel’s 700th anniversary)
  - “Cup of coffee” framing seen as counterproductive: devalues the gift and loads the language

### Gaps and Pain Points for Smaller Teams

- Prospect research: most small college teams lack a dedicated person
  - Full-time prospect research (Companies House, LinkedIn, charity trustee records) is high-value but hard to justify
  - Enables warm, targeted cultivation (e.g., seating a donor next to a music scholar after finding their Royal Opera House trusteeship)
- Legacy giving officers: same problem; hard to justify the headcount, but returns can be transformational
- Fund proliferation: colleges often accumulate too many small restricted funds (e.g., 108 funds)
  - External audit to rationalise down to ~7 is genuinely useful
- Donor reporting: tension between what donors want (specific impact) and what colleges need (discretionary funds)
  - Creative language is widespread; fundraising regulator scrutiny is increasing
- Regulatory navigation: the Fundraising Regulator’s Code of Practice is non-statutory but causes significant confusion
  - GDPR/PECR intersections (e.g., can you email someone who withdrew before graduating?) are frequently asked but rarely answered clearly
  - Suggested opportunity: a closed GPT trained on the regulatory code, similar to an HR bot

### Consultants and the Competitive Landscape

- Existing consultants (e.g., Hubbub, MORE Partnership) are generalist and struggle with Oxford’s specific context
  - Oxford/Cambridge require specialist knowledge; most consultants’ experience is with larger, less affiliated student bodies
  - Colleges increasingly running giving days in-house; the software is accessible and cheap
- Full-stack consultant bundles (mailing + calling + software) are expensive and often redundant for teams with in-house capability
- AI wealth screening tools offered by consultants are seen as too generic to be useful at college level

### Technology Trends and Future Outlook

- Telethons predicted to decline: trust in phone calls eroding (scam awareness, AI voice mimicry)
  - Likely shift toward thank-you calls only, with giving following from warm relationships
- Hubbub or similar platforms expected to offer AI callers within a few years
  - Will work for large universities; unlikely to work for colleges where personal relationship is the product
- AI-generated marketing already detectable and generating backlash: same icons, same tone, no differentiation
- In-person engagement predicted to grow in value as digital noise increases
  - College affiliation events (dinners, barbecues, on-site visits) will outperform emails and calls
- Horizon-watching and foresight consultancy flagged as a genuine gap: someone who tracks downstream AI impact on fundraising practice
